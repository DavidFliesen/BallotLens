About the Data

Party-Line Voting, explained

A party-line score is the share of recorded votes where a legislator voted the same way as the majority of their own party. Political scientists call it a party-unity score. A high number means they vote with their party most of the time; a lower number means they cross the aisle more often.

Crossing the aisle is not automatically good or bad. It is just information. BallotLens shows the same measure for every party, so a Republican who often votes with Democrats and a Democrat who often votes with Republicans are shown the exact same way. Colloquial nicknames like RINO or DINO refer loosely to this idea, but the number itself is what is verifiable.

Only people who currently hold or have held a legislative office have a roll-call record. Candidates for executive offices (governor, attorney general) or first-time candidates will not have one. Records for Congress come from VoteView; records for the state legislature come from OpenStates.

Where every fact comes from

BallotLens is built so that every factual claim about a real person comes from an official or nonpartisan public source, linked on the item. Explanations are written in plain English from those sources.

  • What is on the ballot: South Carolina Election Commission (scvotes.gov) and Ballotpedia.
  • Party affiliation and basic biography: Ballotpedia, Wikidata, and candidate filings.
  • Congressional voting records and party-unity scores: VoteView (voteview.com), which publishes downloadable roll-call data.
  • State legislature voting records: OpenStates (openstates.org) bulk data.
  • Office and candidate explanations: written in plain English directly from the sources above, not generated by an AI model on the fly.

The data shipped in v001 is a small starter set for the 2026 South Carolina races and is meant to be expanded from the sources above. Treat anything marked illustrative as a placeholder, not a real figure.
